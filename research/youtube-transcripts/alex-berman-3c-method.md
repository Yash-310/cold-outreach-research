### The 3C Method Breakdown

**C1: Compliment**
Open with something specific and genuine about the prospect. Not generic flattery — a concrete observation.
- Reference their YouTube video view count
- Mention a specific product feature you noticed
- Cite a recent company milestone

The compliment serves two purposes: it proves you did research, and it creates a micro-moment of goodwill before the ask.

**C2: Case Study**
Share a relevant result you've achieved for a similar company. The ideal case study:
- Names a recognizable company (or competitor)
- Includes a specific metric (%, $, timeframe)
- Relates to a problem the prospect likely has

Example pattern: "We helped [COMPANY] increase [METRIC] by [NUMBER] in [TIMEFRAME]."

Berman's insight: even uninterested prospects reply to see what their competitors are doing. The case study creates curiosity independent of buying intent.

**C3: Call to Action**
End with a single, binary question. The CTA should be answerable with "yes" or "no."
- Good: "Worth a 15-minute call next week?"
- Bad: "Let me know if you're interested"
- Bad: Calendar link in first email

Binary CTAs reduce decision fatigue. A calendar link in the first email feels presumptuous. A yes/no question feels conversational.

### Additional Insights from Berman's YouTube Content

**Finding qualified leads**: Uses BuiltWith to find companies by technology stack, LinkedIn to identify decision-makers, and job boards for buying signals (e.g., a company hiring designers indicates they need design tools). More qualifying factors = higher lead quality.

**Qualifying questions over scripts**: Scripts feel robotic. Questions like "have you used something like this before?" and "why did you stop?" reveal where the prospect is in their buying journey.

**Touchpoints**: B2B SaaS requires 5-7 touchpoints across multiple channels. Follow-up emails every two days, combined with LinkedIn engagement and Twitter interaction.

**Start at the CEO**: Berman starts outreach at the CEO level, even at large companies (Morgan Stanley, Nike). If the CEO turns it down, they often route you to the right department. Starting too low means you get ignored. Starting at the top means you get redirected with an internal referral.
